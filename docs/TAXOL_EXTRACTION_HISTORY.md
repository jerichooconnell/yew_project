# Pacific Yew (Taxus brevifolia) & Taxol: Historical Harvest Summary

## Background
The Pacific Yew (_Taxus brevifolia_) was historically considered a "trash tree" by the logging industry until the 1960s, when the National Cancer Institute (NCI) discovered that its bark contained a powerful anti-cancer compound called paclitaxel (marketed as Taxol). This discovery triggered a massive and controversial "yew-rush" in the Pacific Northwest forests.

## Harvest Numbers & Ecological Impact
Because paclitaxel is found primarily in the thin bark of the yew, harvesting the compound required stripping the bark, which inherently killed the tree. The yield of the active ingredient was astonishingly low, leading to staggering harvest quotas:

* **Yield Ratios:** It took approximately **20,000 pounds (9,080 kg)** of yew bark to produce a single kilogram (2.2 lbs) of pure Taxol.
* **Patient Cost in Trees:** Producing enough Taxol to treat a single cancer patient required the bark of roughly **six 100+-year-old mature yew trees**.
* **Tree Mortality:** To meet the anticipated clinical demand for the drug in the early 1990s, the NCI estimated that **360,000 mature yew trees per year** would need to be harvested.
* **Scale of Harvest:** 
    * In 1988 alone, the NCI procured 60,000 pounds of bark, requiring the destruction of **3,000 to 3,500 trees**, yielding a mere 19 pounds of Taxol.
    * By 1991, the US Forest Service allowed Hauser Northwest (the primary bark collector for Bristol-Myers Squibb) to harvest over **825,000 pounds** of bark from Pacific Northwest national forests in a single year.
    * Over the course of the peak harvest period (roughly 1989-1993), **hundreds of thousands of ancient Pacific Yow trees** were stripped and killed. At one point, Bristol-Myers Squibb was permitted to cut down an additional 38,000 trees just for one operational push.

## Resolution
The unsustainable harvest of wild Pacific Yews sparked massive outcry among environmentalists, as the old-growth forests that harbored these slow-growing understory trees were being rapidly decimated. 

By 1993, the crisis was averted when researchers successfully developed semi-synthetic pathways to produce paclitaxel using 10-deacetylbaccatin III (10-DAB)—a precursor compound found in the needles of the European Yew (_Taxus baccata_). Because needles can be responsibly pruned without killing the tree, the large-scale slaughter of wild Pacific Yew populations in North America to produce Taxol largely ceased. Currently, paclitaxel is also produced via plant cell fermentation.
