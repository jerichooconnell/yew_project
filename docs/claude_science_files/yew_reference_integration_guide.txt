**Insert after existing description of background-sample generation:**

The choice of background (negative) samples is critical for the reliability of 
presence‐only SDMs (Barber et al., 2021). We selected pseudo‐absences from within 
the known biogeographic range of Taxus brevifolia and areas with suitable substrate 
(forest, excluding alpine and water) rather than from the broader study region, 
following target‐group methodology to avoid bias introduced by incomplete survey 
effort in peripheral areas (Barber et al., 2021). This approach reduces false 
negatives arising from unsampled suitable habitat and improves model specificity 
for the habitat conditions under which yew actually occurs.

**Current problem:** Reports AUC = 0.996 without caveating spatial autocorrelation

**Option A (insert in Methods, validation subsection):**

Model validation relied on spatial cross-validation, but we note a critical 
caveat: tile‐based habitat classification can spuriously inflate accuracy metrics 
due to spatial autocorrelation in the underlying satellite data (Ploton et al., 
2020). Because the 98 habitat-quality tiles are clustered across a small fraction 
of the study zone (~0.26% coverage), model AUC values may overstate true 
generalization to unsampled regions. A stricter spatially-stratified cross-validation 
that excludes adjacent tiles from training-test splits would provide a more 
conservative (and likely lower) accuracy bound (Ploton et al., 2020). The reported 
AUC = 0.996 should be interpreted as conditional on the sampled tile locations 
rather than as province-wide performance.

**Option B (insert in Results, after AUC statement):**

The XGBoost classifier achieved AUC = 0.996 on spatially-independent test folds. 
However, this high accuracy likely reflects in part the within-tile spatial 
autocorrelation of satellite features, a widespread issue in large-area ecological 
mapping (Ploton et al., 2020). Because habitat quality was assessed on spatially 
clustered tiles (~0.26% of the study zone), true generalization to unexplored 
regions may be lower. Strict spatial-clustering validation (e.g., leave-one-tile-out 
cross-validation) would be needed to bound the classifier's predictive scope (Ploton 
et al., 2020).

Our habitat-decline estimate (69.2% over the reference period) serves as evidence 
for IUCN Red List Criterion A2c, which requires demonstration of a continuing 
decline in area of habitat, extent of occurrence, or quality of habitat. Recent 
work on standardized, semi-automated remote-sensing-based IUCN assessment (Cazalis 
et al., 2024) emphasizes the importance of explicitly documenting: (1) the baseline 
against which decline is measured, (2) the sources and spatial coverage of remote 
sensing data, and (3) the quantitative assumptions linking habitat loss to 
population decline. Our analysis addresses the first two transparently, but the 
third—converting habitat loss to population consequence—was previously qualitative. 
The stage-structured demographic projection presented in Section [X] bridges this

area burned relative to the 20-year baseline would be required to sustain the 
observed 69% decline over one IUCN generation (80–100 years). This translates the 
remote-sensing-derived habitat loss into a testable, mechanistic population model, 
consistent with emerging best practices (sRedList; Cazalis et al., 2024) and 
strengthens the biological justification for the A2c assessment.
